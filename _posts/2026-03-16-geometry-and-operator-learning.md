---
title: "Why Geometry Matters in Operator Learning"
date: 2026-03-16
---
I keep returning to the same complaint when I look at a lot of machine-learning work on PDEs: the mathematics of the problem is often acknowledged in the motivation section and then quietly dropped in the architecture. We say the operator is local. We say the domain has geometry. We say conservation matters. And then we hand the data to a model that behaves as though every point is just another token in a generic exchangeable set. Sometimes that still works numerically, and I do not want to dismiss that. But it leaves me with the feeling that the model succeeded despite the formulation, not because the formulation respected the object being learned.

That is why geometry matters in operator learning, at least to me. The issue is not only computational efficiency. The issue is fidelity to the structure of the target. If I want to learn an operator

$$
\mathcal{G}_{\theta}: a(x) \mapsto u(x),
$$

and if the true map depends in a structured way on locality, domain geometry, and physically meaningful neighborhoods, then a model that begins by pretending all points are equally plausible interaction partners is already making a strong and possibly wrong assumption. It may later learn to undo that mistake, but why ask it to start from the wrong worldview?

I think this becomes especially clear in fluid or field settings. If I am learning a time-advance operator or a map between fields, I am not really solving a sequence problem. I am not writing language. I am not predicting the next token in an arbitrary line. I am approximating a map between functions defined on a domain. The coordinates are not decoration. The adjacency structure is not an implementation convenience. The geometry belongs to the problem in the same way the symbols belong to algebra.

This is one reason I have become more interested in geometry-aware sparse attention. The phrase can sound like a trendy architectural variation, but I do not think that is the right way to understand it. For me, the deeper claim is that sparse attention should emerge from a scientific hypothesis about admissible interaction. If the operator is locally coupled, then the neighborhoods over which information is mixed should reflect that. The model should not first assume an all-to-all social universe and then learn, expensively, that most of the conversation was never necessary.

That is the intuition behind treating tokens as geometric objects rather than bare feature vectors. If a token is something like \(\tau_i^t = (x_i, u_i^t)\), then the location is already part of the state. Once that is true, positional encoding starts looking less like a principled necessity and more like a workaround for having represented the problem in the wrong coordinate language. I do not mean that every model must follow this exact route. I mean that when geometry is constitutive, it should appear at the beginning of the formulation, not as an apology added later.

The graph view makes the point even sharper. A radius graph or k-nearest-neighbor graph is not just a sparse data structure. It is a statement about which interactions are even eligible to matter. That matters computationally, of course, because the cost profile changes from dense \(O(N^2)\) interaction to something more like \(O(Nk)\). But I think the scientific reason is more important. The graph encodes a belief about locality. It says that the operator’s dependence is structured, and the model should be forced to live inside that structure unless there is a reason to relax it.

There is a recurring misunderstanding in ML that inductive bias is somehow the opposite of flexibility, as though structure always means dogma. I do not see it that way. A good inductive bias is a way of refusing to waste capacity on relationships that should have been ruled out by understanding. The world is not fully dense just because a GPU can multiply dense matrices. Sometimes sparsity is not a concession to resource limits. It is a closer description of the phenomenon.

This is where I find the language of learned local quadrature especially satisfying. When a neighborhood-based attention rule approximates a local integral operator, I feel like the architecture is finally speaking the right dialect. It is no longer merely “mixing features.” It is approximating structured interaction over a domain. The geometry is visible in the rule. The neighborhood is visible in the rule. The model can still learn expressive weights, but it learns them inside a framework that has already admitted something true about the target class.

I also think geometry matters because evaluation becomes more honest once the model is structured correctly. A dense model can sometimes produce very good one-step numbers while learning an internal habit that does not survive longer rollout or domain variation. A geometry-aware model can still fail, obviously, but its failures tend to be more interpretable. If neighborhood size is wrong, if boundary handling is weak, or if the representation is not respecting the domain, I usually get a clearer signal of where to look. Scientific machine learning should value that clarity more than it currently does.

There is a philosophical reason for this that I keep circling back to. A Newtonian way of thinking about science says that explanation matters at the level of form, not just output. Newton did not make mechanics powerful by listing observed trajectories one by one. He made it powerful by placing motion under a structured law. In a much smaller computational setting, I think operator learning deserves the same kind of seriousness. We should ask not only whether a network can hit the targets, but whether the form of the network expresses a defensible idea about the operator. If the answer is no, then even a good benchmark result feels thin.

That is also why I do not like hearing sparse attention described here as if it were merely an optimization trick. Optimization is part of the story, but it is not the heart of it. The heart of it is epistemic discipline. What does the model assume can interact? Why does it assume that? What kind of locality is being encoded? These questions sit much closer to scientific modeling than to ordinary architecture shopping.

Of course, respecting geometry is not enough on its own. A model can be geometrically informed and still scientifically shallow if the losses, data contract, or evaluation protocol are weak. I care about divergence penalties, energy-sensitive consistency, and rollout quality for exactly that reason. A model that knows where points are but does not care what physically meaningful structure it destroys is only halfway serious. Geometry is a necessary piece of the story, not the whole story.

But it is still one of the first pieces I want in place, because it changes the entire atmosphere of the problem. Once geometry is built in, the model is forced to reckon with the domain instead of floating above it. The architecture becomes less generic and, in my view, more honest. And that honesty can sometimes make the model cheaper as well. I like that combination. It suggests that computational efficiency and scientific realism do not always pull in opposite directions. Sometimes the right structure is both more faithful and more efficient.

So when I say geometry matters in operator learning, I do not mean only that coordinates can help accuracy. I mean that geometry changes what kind of object the model believes it is learning. That is a deeper claim, and I think it is the one worth defending.
