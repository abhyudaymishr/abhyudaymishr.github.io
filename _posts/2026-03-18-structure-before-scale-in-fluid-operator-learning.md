---
title: "Structure Before Scale in Fluid Operator Learning"
date: 2026-03-18
---
One of the easiest mistakes to make in fluid operator learning is to confuse larger experiments with deeper understanding. The field is now rich enough that almost any architectural paper can present attractive plots, strong one-step errors, and a convincing story about expressive power. But the more time I spend with Navier-Stokes style systems, the less satisfied I am with that style of success. Fluid dynamics pushes back against shallow methodology. If the model has not learned something structurally right, long-horizon rollout, domain shift, or conservation-sensitive evaluation eventually exposes it.

That is why I keep coming back to a simple principle: structure before scale. Before asking how large the model is, how long it was trained, or how many samples it saw, I want to know whether the experimental setting respected the mathematical structure of the problem. Did the representation preserve the geometry? Was locality treated seriously? Were rollout metrics considered, or only one-step prediction? Was the data contract documented clearly enough that another person could reproduce it? Were the baselines implemented with equal care? Without good answers to those questions, scale becomes a kind of camouflage.

I do not mean that large experiments are unimportant. Of course they matter. But in scientific machine learning, size without structure often creates a false sense of inevitability. A model may look dominant because the benchmark rewards exactly the kind of shortcut the architecture is predisposed to exploit. Another may look worse simply because it was evaluated under slightly different preprocessing or rollout conditions. These are not minor procedural quirks. They shape the meaning of the result.

Navier-Stokes problems make this visible very quickly. A model can have excellent one-step loss and still become unreliable when iterated. It can preserve local texture while quietly drifting in physically important quantities. It can appear stable on a narrow benchmark regime and then fall apart when the geometry or boundary behavior changes. This is why I am suspicious of presentations that flatten fluid operator learning into a single score. Fluids punish shortcuts, but sometimes only after a delay.

Long-horizon rollout is therefore not a luxury metric to add at the end if there is time. It is one of the main tests of whether the operator has learned something durable. If a learned map compounds error in a way that destroys the qualitative behavior of the system, then its one-step success is much less impressive than it first appeared. The same is true for conservation-sensitive quantities. I do not expect every learned operator to obey physical structure perfectly, but I do expect the evaluation to make visible what is being preserved and what is being lost.

This is also why I care so much about data contracts. If the dataset is described ambiguously, if normalization is underdocumented, if the geometric meaning of the coordinates is unclear, or if the train-test split changes silently across runs, then the model comparison becomes unstable before the first gradient step. Scientific machine learning inherits a bad habit from mainstream ML here: people often talk as if the dataset were simply given. In operator learning, that is rarely true. The representation of the field, the discretization, the target map, and the rollout protocol are all part of the scientific statement.

The same is true of baselines. A flashy architecture should not be compared against a baseline that was implemented casually. If the new method gets careful tuning, structure-preserving losses, and thoughtful preprocessing while the baseline is treated as a ritual obligation, then the result says more about authorial attention than about the models. I do not think this is malicious most of the time. It is just common. But it weakens the field’s ability to tell which improvements are real.

This is part of why I separate the broader Navier-Stokes operator program from any single model such as GOLA. GOLA is one answer to a specific architectural question about geometry-induced sparse attention. The larger research program is a methodological one. How should operator-learning systems for fluids actually be studied if the goal is not only to interpolate data, but to approximate a scientifically meaningful map? That is a harder question, and I think it is the one worth staying with.

There is a philosophical lens that helps me here, and it is a Galilean one. Galileo’s contribution was not merely to accumulate observations of motion. It was to insist that motion becomes intelligible when described in the right mathematical form. I think something similar should guide learned operators. A model should not be judged only by whether it produces plausible frames. It should be judged by whether its formulation and behavior line up with the structural language of the system it is modeling.

That is why reproducibility is not a bureaucratic afterthought. It belongs to the epistemology of the project. If a result cannot survive rerunning, reimplementation, or careful ablation, then it has not yet earned scientific trust. In research areas where code, data, and architecture are tightly entangled, reproducibility is not separate from truth-seeking. It is one of the ways truth-seeking is operationalized.

I also think structure before scale is a useful personal discipline. It keeps me from being seduced too quickly by large runs, crowded figures, or attractive tables. It forces me back to first questions. What exactly is the operator? What assumptions does the architecture encode? What kind of locality is being assumed? How do errors behave under iteration? Which quantities matter physically, and are they being monitored? Those questions are not glamorous, but they usually tell me more than another round of training ever could.

There is a paradox here that I like. Respecting structure can sometimes make a model cheaper as well as better grounded. A local or geometry-aware architecture may reduce computation precisely because it stops pretending the problem is more entangled than it really is. In that sense, structure is not the enemy of practical performance. Sometimes it is the precondition for meaningful performance.

So when I say structure before scale in fluid operator learning, I am not arguing against ambition. I am arguing for ordering ambition correctly. First make the representation, the benchmark, the losses, and the evaluation scientifically legible. Then scale if scaling still seems necessary. Otherwise the field risks producing very large demonstrations of very unclear claims. For a subject as mathematically serious as fluid dynamics, that would be an unfortunate outcome.
