---
title: "Why Build a Small Language Model by Hand"
date: 2026-03-18
---
I think there is a quiet intellectual value in building a small language model by hand that gets lost in the current culture around large models. The usual assumption is that smaller means less serious, less impressive, maybe even less educational. I increasingly think the opposite can be true. A small system often teaches more precisely because it cannot hide behind scale. The moment the model is compact enough, every other part of the pipeline becomes visible again.

That visibility matters. In language modeling, people often speak as though the model were the main event and everything else were support infrastructure. But tokenizer design, corpus quality, data splitting, baseline choice, evaluation logic, and runtime behavior all shape what the model is actually doing. In a large opaque stack, those things tend to disappear into “the setup.” In a small project, they come back into focus. That is one of the main reasons I wanted to build NanoTransformer as a complete system instead of just a network definition.

By “complete system,” I mean something very ordinary and very important. The repository covers tokenizer training, tokenized dataset preparation, configuration-driven training, checkpoint evaluation, a trigram baseline with add-k smoothing, and latency measurement. I like having all of these pieces present because they turn the project into an end-to-end experiment rather than an isolated architectural sketch. I can follow the path from raw text to token IDs to checkpoints to measured inference behavior without changing mental gears or pretending that preprocessing and evaluation belong to somebody else’s job.

The tokenizer is a good example of why this matters. In a lot of model discussions, the tokenizer is treated as settled plumbing. But tokenization is not neutral. It shapes sequence length, vocabulary pressure, and the structure of what the model is even allowed to see. When I train a BPE tokenizer explicitly and then build the dataset from it, I am forced to confront those choices. I am reminded that the model does not learn on language in the abstract. It learns on the particular symbolic interface I have given it.

The same is true of baselines. I prefer projects that include a baseline simple enough to be slightly embarrassing if the main model fails to outperform it decisively. In NanoTransformer, that means a trigram baseline with smoothing. I do not include it for nostalgia. I include it because it keeps the experiment honest. If a transformer-based system is only marginally better than a classical baseline under the current setup, then that is valuable information. It means either the task is narrow, the implementation is weak, or the claimed gain is smaller than the fashionable architecture would suggest.

Latency measurement serves a similar role. It keeps the project from drifting into a purely symbolic success story. A model that trains and evaluates well but behaves poorly at inference time is not a complete achievement. Even for a tiny model, measuring latency makes the project answer to the real world. It reminds me that software systems live in time, not only in loss curves.

There is also a methodological reason I trust small projects more when I am trying to learn. A Descartes-like rule suggests that when a system is too large to understand, the right move is not always to make it larger. Sometimes the right move is to reduce it until the mechanism becomes inspectable. A tiny transformer is useful because it allows that inspection. I can see what changes when I alter the tokenizer. I can see the effect of a baseline choice. I can trace a bug without needing a research cluster or a massive serving stack.

This is not anti-scale moralizing. Large models have their place. But scale can create a false aura of explanation. Once a model is enormous, there is a temptation to treat performance as self-justifying. Weak baselines, vague preprocessing, or underexamined engineering choices become harder to notice because the overall result still looks impressive. A small model refuses that comfort. It asks for a cleaner argument.

I also think small systems are good for preserving the link between theory and implementation. It is easy to say “we built a transformer” as though that were already informative. But what configuration? What context length? What tokenization? What dataset contract? What evaluation protocol? What runtime? These details stop being footnotes when the project is small enough that they dominate the outcome. That is healthy. It means the experiment remains made of parts a human can still reason about.

For me personally, building a small LM by hand also serves as a counterweight to more concept-heavy work. In areas like operator learning, topology, or qudit simulation, it is easy to live at a high level of abstraction for too long. A compact language-model project drags me back into software reality. Files have to line up. Artifacts have to be named coherently. Baselines have to run. Configs have to make sense. Measurements have to be taken. That grounding is useful. It prevents research taste from drifting away from engineering truth.

I think this is why I resist calling projects like this “just educational.” They are educational, but that is not a dismissal. A good educational system is often a better research instrument than a huge opaque one, because it preserves causal visibility. When something works, I can often tell why. When something fails, I can usually isolate the layer of failure. That is much harder in larger settings where every problem immediately turns into a systems archaeology exercise.

There is another practical advantage. Small complete systems age well. A project that includes training, evaluation, baseline comparison, and latency tooling is much easier to extend than one that only contains the model core. Future work can grow from it rather than replace it wholesale. That matters to me because I like repositories that remain usable as thinking tools, not just as snapshots of one experiment.

So when I ask why build a small language model by hand, my answer is not “because it is cute” or “because everyone should start small.” My answer is that small systems restore proportion. They force the experiment to become answerable. They make tokenization, baselines, and runtime visible again. They lower the distance between idea and mechanism. And in a field where scale often outruns understanding, that still feels like a serious reason to build.
